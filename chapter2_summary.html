Section 1 — An Attractive Mineral

How a Magnet Works in Symmetric Field Terms

An iron bar magnet is immersed in the ST Field like everything else. Inside the bar, iron atoms form long lines from one end to the other. These atoms oscillate and act like propellers on a motor boat — they push the ST Field out one end and pull it in the other. Between the atomic lines, the ST Field fills all the space.

The shape and spin of the iron atoms determine the field's directional preference. The T Field is driven out the North end of the magnet, and the S Field is driven out the South end. The number of atomic lines and the density of the magnet determine how far the surrounding fields are affected.

Because it is the nature of T Field to CLOSE in on itself (to “take its own tail in its mouth”), the T Field emerging from the North end seeks to rejoin itself near the South end. It forms a circuit loop — through the magnet, around the outside, back through again. Each passage through one atomic line may form a separate circuit. Meanwhile, S Field swirls in the opposite direction, forming spreading sheaths around each T Field line, giving it definition and separating one line from another. Bundles of lines spread outward in an apple shape around the magnet.

Vital Engineering Principle Force is only functional when it confronts something “like-unlike” or equal and opposite to itself. Force acts upon fields of its own octave speeds and is not noticeable as force in other octaves. This fact is vitally important to all engineers.
Section 2 — Magnet Interactions

Why Magnets Attract and “Repel”

N-to-N or S-to-S: Opposing directional flows of the same field type butt against each other. The flows are pushed aside to prevent collision. This creates the sensation of repelling, but it is actually opposing field flows being diverted — not true repulsion.

N-to-S: The lines of force are fully compatible. T Fields CLOSE in the normal way, S Fields continue wrapping around each T Field line. One unified circuit forms instead of two, and a single field sphere encompasses the whole.

An important observation: this harmonic integration is slow. If you pass a small magnet rapidly and perpendicularly through the force field of a larger magnet, it shows little attraction. Moved very slowly, the attraction becomes evident. The whole of each sphere has to sense the harmonies of the other and agree to form a common sphere and axis. This slow harmonic integration plays a vital role in engineering.

Note also: an iron magnet does not exert force on a bar of copper or brass because their atomic structures differ — they do not have compatible vibrations. Force is unique to its own octave. However, gravity engages all materials because gravity is a grand overtone of oscillations.
